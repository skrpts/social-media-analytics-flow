## Social Media Metrics Reference Guide

This reference provides definitions, formulas, benchmarks, and interpretation guidance for the key metrics used throughout the social media analytics flow.

### Core Engagement Metrics

**Engagement Rate**
- **Formula:** (Total Engagements / Total Impressions) x 100
- **Alternative:** (Total Engagements / Followers) x 100 (for follower-based rate)
- **What it measures:** How actively the audience interacts with content relative to how many people see it
- **Interpretation:** Higher is better, but compare within platform and industry — a 3% engagement rate on LinkedIn is strong; on TikTok it is below average

**Reach**
- **Definition:** Unique accounts that saw the content
- **Differs from impressions:** Impressions count multiple views by the same account
- **What it measures:** Audience breadth — how many distinct people your content reaches
- **Interpretation:** Compare to follower count. Reach consistently above follower count indicates strong discovery/sharing

**Impressions**
- **Definition:** Total number of times content was displayed, including repeat views
- **What it measures:** Content visibility volume
- **Interpretation:** High impressions with low engagement suggests the content is seen but not compelling enough to act on

**Click-Through Rate (CTR)**
- **Formula:** (Link Clicks / Impressions) x 100
- **What it measures:** Effectiveness at driving traffic from social to a destination
- **Interpretation:** Varies heavily by platform and content type. Benchmark against your own historical data rather than industry averages

### Engagement Type Hierarchy

Not all engagements are equal. Ranked by value:

1. **Saves/Bookmarks** — Highest value. User found content worth returning to. Indicates genuine utility.
2. **Shares/Reposts** — High value. User willing to associate their personal brand with your content. Amplifies reach.
3. **Comments** — High value (if substantive). Indicates the content provoked thought or emotion. Algorithmic boost on most platforms.
4. **Replies to comments** — Very high value. Indicates community building and conversation depth.
5. **Likes/Reactions** — Low value individually but signals baseline resonance. Easy action, low commitment.
6. **Profile visits** — Moderate value. Indicates curiosity beyond the post — potential follower conversion.

### Derived Metrics Worth Tracking

**Amplification Rate**
- **Formula:** Shares / Total Posts
- **Meaning:** How much your audience multiplies your reach by sharing content

**Conversation Rate**
- **Formula:** Comments / Total Posts
- **Meaning:** How much your content prompts discussion

**Save Rate**
- **Formula:** Saves / Impressions x 100
- **Meaning:** What proportion of viewers found the content valuable enough to save

**Audience Growth Rate**
- **Formula:** (New Followers - Unfollows) / Total Followers x 100
- **Meaning:** Net audience growth normalised for audience size

**Content Velocity**
- **Formula:** Posts Published / Time Period
- **Meaning:** Publishing cadence — track alongside engagement to find diminishing returns

### Metric Interpretation Pitfalls

1. **Vanity metric fixation** — Follower counts and likes feel good but rarely correlate with business outcomes. Focus on engagement rate, click-through rate, and conversions.

2. **Cross-platform comparison** — Comparing raw engagement numbers across platforms is misleading. A LinkedIn post with 50 comments is far more valuable than an Instagram post with 200 likes.

3. **Algorithmic influence** — Reach and impressions are heavily influenced by platform algorithms. A reach decline may reflect an algorithm change, not a content quality issue.

4. **Small sample bias** — One viral post can skew an entire month's metrics. Use medians alongside averages and look at consistency across multiple posts.

5. **Paid vs. organic conflation** — Always separate paid and organic metrics. Blending them makes organic strategy assessment impossible.

6. **Engagement rate denominator** — Using followers vs. impressions as the denominator produces very different rates. Be consistent and state which formula you use.
