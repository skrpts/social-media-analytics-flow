## Social Media Analytics Guide

This guide provides a practical framework for measuring social media performance, building meaningful reports, and using data to drive content strategy decisions. It covers the full analytics lifecycle from goal setting through data collection, analysis, and action.

### Setting Up for Success

#### Define What Success Looks Like

Before opening an analytics dashboard, answer these questions:
1. **What business objective does social media serve?** (brand awareness, lead generation, community building, customer support, thought leadership)
2. **What specific outcomes would demonstrate success?** (website traffic, email sign-ups, sales pipeline influence, brand search volume, NPS improvement)
3. **What time horizon are you measuring?** (weekly content performance, monthly strategy assessment, quarterly business impact)

These answers determine which metrics matter and which are noise.

#### Choose Your Primary Metrics

Select 3-5 primary metrics aligned with your business objective:

| Business Objective | Primary Metrics |
|-------------------|----------------|
| Brand awareness | Reach, impressions, follower growth rate, share of voice |
| Lead generation | Click-through rate, conversion rate, cost per lead |
| Community building | Comment rate, conversation rate, community growth, repeat engager rate |
| Customer support | Response time, resolution rate, sentiment score |
| Thought leadership | Share rate, save rate, profile visits, earned media mentions |

Resist tracking everything. Five well-understood metrics beat twenty poorly understood ones.

### Data Collection

#### Platform Native Analytics

Every major platform provides free analytics:
- **LinkedIn:** Page Analytics and individual post analytics (Company Pages only)
- **Instagram:** Professional Dashboard and Insights (Business/Creator accounts only)
- **X/Twitter:** Analytics dashboard and individual tweet analytics
- **TikTok:** Creator Tools analytics
- **YouTube:** YouTube Studio analytics

**Strengths:** Free, accurate, real-time data
**Weaknesses:** Platform-siloed, limited historical data, no cross-platform view

#### Third-Party Analytics Tools

For cross-platform reporting and deeper analysis, consider tools such as:
- Sprout Social, Hootsuite, or Buffer for cross-platform dashboards
- Brandwatch or Mention for social listening and sentiment analysis
- Google Analytics for tracking social traffic to your website

**When to use:** When managing 3+ platforms, when you need historical trend data beyond what platforms retain, or when you need automated reporting.

### The Analysis Process

#### Weekly Quick Check (15 minutes)

Review these numbers weekly to catch issues early:
1. Engagement rate — is it stable, trending up, or trending down?
2. Best-performing post — what made it work?
3. Worst-performing post — what went wrong?
4. Follower growth — any unusual gains or losses?
5. Any comments or messages requiring attention?

This is not a report — it is a pulse check. Note anything unusual for deeper investigation.

#### Monthly Performance Report (1-2 hours)

Run the performance-report-generator prompt with the month's data. The report should cover:
1. Key metrics with month-over-month and year-over-year comparison
2. Top and bottom performing content with analysis
3. Audience growth and composition changes
4. Content pillar performance comparison
5. Engagement pattern shifts
6. Recommendations for the coming month

Distribute to the marketing team. Store reports consistently for trend analysis.

#### Quarterly Strategic Review (half day)

The quarterly review goes deeper:
1. Run the full analytics pipeline (all 5 stages of the workflow)
2. Assess whether the social media strategy is delivering against business objectives
3. Review audience profiles and update if the audience has shifted
4. Evaluate platform performance and resource allocation
5. Set goals and strategy adjustments for the next quarter

Present to marketing leadership with data-backed recommendations.

### Making Data Actionable

The gap between data and action is where most analytics programmes fail. Follow this framework:

#### The Insight-to-Action Bridge

For every insight, complete this sentence:
> "Because [data insight], we should [specific action], which we expect will [measurable outcome]."

Examples:
- "Because carousel posts generate 3x the save rate of single images, we should increase carousel frequency from 2 to 4 per week, which we expect will increase overall save rate by 15%."
- "Because engagement drops 40% on Fridays, we should shift Friday content to Monday morning, which we expect will improve weekly engagement rate by 5-8%."

If you cannot complete this sentence, the insight is not actionable yet.

#### Common Analysis Mistakes

1. **Reporting without recommending** — A report that says "engagement dropped 15%" without explaining why or what to do about it is useless.
2. **Confusing correlation with causation** — Posting at 9am correlates with higher engagement, but that might be because you post your best content at 9am, not because 9am is magic.
3. **Ignoring context** — A 20% engagement drop during a holiday week is not alarming. A 20% drop during a product launch is.
4. **Optimising for the wrong metric** — Maximising impressions is easy (post provocative content). Maximising valuable engagement is harder and more useful.
5. **Analysis paralysis** — Do not wait for perfect data. Make decisions on directionally correct data and iterate.

### Building a Testing Culture

The best social media teams test constantly. Establish a simple testing framework:

1. **Hypothesis:** "We believe [change] will improve [metric] because [reasoning]"
2. **Test design:** Change one variable at a time. Run for at least 2 weeks or 10+ posts.
3. **Measurement:** Compare test content against control content on the primary metric.
4. **Decision:** If the hypothesis is confirmed, adopt the change. If not, document the learning and move on.

Test ideas to get started:
- Posting time optimisation (same content, different days/times)
- Caption length (short punchy vs. long detailed)
- Visual style (photography vs. graphics vs. user-generated)
- Call-to-action phrasing (question vs. statement vs. instruction)
- Hashtag volume (5 vs. 15 vs. 25 hashtags)
